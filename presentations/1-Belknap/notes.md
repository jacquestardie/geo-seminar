# Rodriguez: Bay-head deltas

Bay-head delta: _A delta at the head of an estuary or a bay into which a river discharges because of the margin of the land’s late partial submergence._

Gulf of Mexico estuaries show rapid landward shifts around the 8.2ka event.

> Estuaries friend by low-gradient shorelines are highly sensitive to low-amplitude and sudden sea-level rise.

Holocene: _Current geological epoch_

Time scales worth taking a look at: Age < Epoch < Period

Back stepping: rapid landward shift.

> Although an increase in the rate of sea-level rise was inferred as the forcing mechanism, the underlying cause for the sea-level jump was unknown because of poor correlation of the back-stepping events with existing sea-level curves.

> Decadal to century-scale sea-level rises of decimeter magnitude are expected from episodic and localized retreat of ice sheets... [Especially early in the Holocene]

> Bay-head deltas are sensitive to small changes in accommodation, which are strongly controlled by sea level, making this depositional environment ideal for [examination]... This sensitively is because bay-head deltas have extremely low gradients along depositional dip, and are confined within flooded paleo-valleys.

Flooding-surfaces.

Back stepping mechanisms

> The increase in the rate of sea-level rise would have manifested itself as a flooding surface and a landward shift in depositional environments within estuaries. If estuarine sediment supply was constant during this time period, an increase in the rate of sea-level rise would expand the area of the estuary and and the space available for deposition. This would cause sedimentation rates at any single estuarine location to decrease.
