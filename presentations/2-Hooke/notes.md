## Brown: Peak Water

It requires ~2000l of water to produce the food each of us consume daily.

> A future of water shortages will also be a future of food shortages.

Irrigated area/person is 10% less today than in 1960. **Not sure if this is just the US or what.**

An increasing number of wells is draining *fossil aquifers* more and more rapidly. These aquifers are not naturally replenished.

In 2008, the Saudi's began phasing out wheat production, as their aquifers had largely been depleted.

**Yemen is becoming Dune.**

> For the first time in history, water shortages are shrinking the grain harvest in an entire geographic region (The Middle East).

~130m Chinese are being fed with grain produced from unsustainable water sources.

## Long: Photosynthesis

Yield potential = seed mass / unit ground area obtained under optimum growing conditions.

Yp dependent on:

1. Available light energy
2. Efficiency of light capture
3. Conversion efficiency
4. Proportion of biomass partitioned into grain.

Most important crops in terms of yield: Maize, Rice, Wheat, Soybeans, Barley, Sorghum

> Photosynthesis is the only remaining major trait available for further increases in Yp on the scale of the last 50 years.

An explanation of the C3 and C4 pathways is [here](http://www.plantphysiol.org/content/155/1/56)

### Arguments against

> Modern bread wheat cultivars have lower leaf photosynthetic rates than their wild ancestors.

> If crop improvement results in increased leaf area, mean leaf photosynthetic rates may decline due to increased self-shading.

> These conclusions that leaf photosynthesis has little potential in increasing crop yields are based on comparisons of different genotypes in which differences in leaf photosynthesis are confounded with many other genetic differences.

### The link between photosynthesis & yield

Doubling of C02 caused:

1. No increase in leaf area
2. A 23-58% increase in leaf photosynthetic rate
3. A 35% increase in crop yield.

A sustained increase in leaf photosynthesis leads to increased crop yield.

### Potential radiation use efficiency

50% of of solar energy is outside the photosynthetically active waveband.

Maximum possible efficiencies are:
- 0.051 in C3 plants
- 0.060 in C4 plants

Observed maxima:
- 0.035 in C3 plants
- 0.043 in C4 plants.

The seemingly low values we see are actually at about 70% of the theoretical maxima.

### Specific opportunities for increasing photosynthesis

The most widely promoted strategies:

1. Conversion of C3 crops to C4.
2. Improved *rubisco* specificity.

... May also be the most difficult to achieve.

#### Modifying crop canopies

Improve the distribution of light on leaf surfaces, so as to mitigate the effect of saturation.

More vertical upper leaves are more efficient in this regard.

#### Relaxing the photoprotected state

> Large gains can be achieved if the lag in relaxation of photo protection can be decreased... [However] Photoprotection fulfills a necessary function of decreasing the probability of oxidative damage,

> Up-regulation of capacity for recovery from photo protection is feasible and may already have been achieved in rice.

#### Photorespiration

As temperature increases, so does photorespiration. Results from the unavoidable oxygenation of RuBP by rubisco.

**Perhaps unnecessary?**

Requires low oxygenation levels, in order to inhibit the oxygenation reaction of rubisco.

#### Overview of opportunities & barriers

Further increase in Yp will depend largely on increasing crop photosynthesis.

> The issue of whether a single or few gene transformations can increase photosynthesis and yield is critical.

Four potential areas:

1. Alteration of crop-canopy architecture to improve the distribution of radiation.
2. Increased recovery from photo protection to increase the efficiency of of photosynthesis of leaves in the shade.
3. Rubisco concentrations, while high, currently limit light-saturated photosynthesis.
4. Increase the capacity of RuBP regeneration.

#### Conclusion

> Crop [improvements] do not appear realizable on a 10-20 years time horizon.


## Madramootoo: Sustainable Groundwater use in Agriculture

> Groundwater irrigation demand has been growing steadily over the past decades, for many reasons including the unreliability of the traditional large canal schemes, and the increasing need of farmers to manage their own irrigation applications.

> The intensification of smallholder agriculture... has further resulted in farmers installing their own low lift pumps to extract shallow groundwater.

> This rapid... expansion of groundwater-based irrigation... has resulted in a complex set of drawdown externalities, including aquifer depletion, migration of low quality water, land subsidence, and loss of environmental services.

> These levels of abstraction have modified the hydrological cycle and augmented levels of evapotranspiration that would otherwise have not occurred, if less groundwater was mobilized.

> Central to the principles of ownership, control, and allocation of groundwater is the creation of a dispute resolution mechanism.
